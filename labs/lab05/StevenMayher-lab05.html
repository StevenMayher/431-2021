Question 2 (10 points)

If we are to assume that the population of interest is the United States as a whole, then no, our sampling would not represent a true random sampling, as the states that were selected were chosen because they were a part of the Midwest. If we assume that the the population of interest is the Midwest, it still wouldnt qualify as a random sample, as all of the counties were selected by state, and not at random from within the region itself. There really isnt a reasonable argument for generalizing the results to the population of all counties in the US either, as again since the counties are all from five specific Midwest states, factors that affect this region of the country could impact the results in a way not found in other regions of the country (weather, for example), however its impossible to know this for certain without clarifying what access to exercise opportunities actually accounts for.

The data examined in Question 1 are independent samples. Question 9 (20 points)

The appropriate cross-tabulation to use here (as specified in Chapter 6 of The Art of Statistics) would be what is known as an error matrix.

Accuracy of the model is the percentage of all survivors and non-survivors that were correctly classified, so in this case the models accuracy = (405 + 9)/(405 + 74 + 9) = 414/488, which is approximately 84.8 %. This means that this model will correctly predict whether or not someone survives about 84.8 % of the time.

The sensitivity of the model is the percentage of the survivors that were correctly predicted by the model, so in this case the sensitivity = 405/(405 + 74) = 405/479, which is approximately 84.6 %. This means that this model correctly predicted a survivor about 84.6 % of the time.

The specificity of the model is the percentage of true non-survivors that were correctly predicted by the model, so in this case the specificity = 9/(9 + 74) = 9/83, which is approximately 10.8 %. This means that this model correctly predicted a non-survivor only about 10.8 % of the time.
